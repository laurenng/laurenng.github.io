Here's Why

First, let me introduce myself

Hi! I'm Lauren Ng, a 4th year student studying Informatics with Honors. I am an outgoing, curious, and passionate student. I am very engaged with the Informatics Program by being a peer advisor, mentor, and program assistant. I also participate in Husky Taekwondo, Dubstech, and work at the Foster Business School. Everyday from 8am to 10pm, I live, breath, and study at UW.


But coming into college, I was the complete opposite. I was a quiet and reclusive Husky. I mearly attended class, did my work in isolation, and spent the majority of my day in my dorm. Luckily, I had the support of my Husky community to bring me out of this shell and transform my into the person I am today! The husky spirit is contagious and I believe it has changed me for the better.

My Origin Story

As mentioned in my introduction, I was never the one to take initiative and do something when things were wrong. The only thing that I had going for me was a handful of upperclassmen mentors I was assigned to from a few RSOs. (Shout out to SWE, HuskyTech, and a2f)


Through invitations to events, encouragement to speak up, and sharing various opportunities, I slowly grew my confidence and satisfaction in my college career. These mentors really took me under their wings and encouraged me to be involved and make something of myself. They showed me what it meant to be a husky and changed my outlook on my college experience.

My First Opportunity

The first opportunity I was blessed with was a really interesting research opportunity with the Informatics program in studying the experiences of Asian and Pacific Islander female students in programming courses. Here, I was placed in a small group to discuss research papers and helped a pHD student with her research. I helped write questions, recruit interviewees, conduct interviews, and write a paper on our findings. In doing this research, we discovered a large majority of female students feeling a sense of not belonging thus being less motivated to succeed. This research opened my mind and gave me more insights into this area of research. It made me realize how big of an issue diversity and the Tech gender gap was. Without this diversity in meetings, there would be opinions left out and overlooked. Thus resulting in designs that potentially appeal to less than half the population.


This journey of research really changed my mindset on communities and finding a sense of belonging. Communities aren’t just formed magically, it takes work and effort. And creating these communities with inclusion and diversity in mind are difficult, but worthwhile to pursue. It also showed me that I can be a leader of change and I became more confident in standing up for what I believe in as I know there are dozens of my peers who silently have the same opinions as me.

Importance of Diversity + Inclusion

In taking HCDE 210, I met a friend, named Nina, who was deaf. She had an ASL interpreter with her everyday in class. One time, the interpreter wasn’t there to perform the ASL translations which she depended on. I didn’t know ASL, but she could read text. So I quickly started typing everything said in class so she would still be involved. It was a small task for me, but it meant the world to Nina. It showed me how small things can make a big difference, especially when it comes to inclusion.


And taking a step back to view the larger picture, diversity is crucial in having diverse perspectives to create more holistic solutions. Some of my best projects have come out of working with individuals who butt heads with me and came from different backgrounds. From working with individuals who have handicapped issues (including stuttering, blindness, and deafness), I have seen firsthand how much value they bring to my community.


When I started planning events for different clubs, I knew providing accessible options was crucial. When planning a virtual design hackathon for Dubstech, my team and I spent a lot of time on making this event accessible. We had a wide-reaching net and attracted over 800+ participants from all over the world. Planning virtual events in the right time zone was difficult and we first directly asked our participants to anonymously tell us what accessible needs they needed. From their input, we ended up hosting 2 introduction meetings with closed captioning and screen-reading options to accommodate as many people as we could.

Another huge aspect of planning this hackathon was inspiring and connecting others

Inspiring others

The beginning of 2021 was rough for everyone, including myself. Many of us were very much isolated and overall motivation was low, especially in the design space. The design hackathon was my way of bringing my community together and rekindling the passion/inspiration many people lost during the pandemic. I had a little over 5 weeks to plan this event, but I knew I had to make it amazing as we had reached hundreds of designers, gather. I worked day and night with my team to plan such an event.


However, if you asked me this 2 years ago if I would be in the position to lead a hackathon event, let alone one that had over 800 participants, I’d think you’re joking. I was never one to take action before becoming into UW and feeling empowered to spread inspiration. The mentors and professors who I’ve met along the way. This ambition to inspire others was from my past mentors and experiences at UW. They took me under their wings and provided me with a wealth of resources and opportunities, it was time I paid it forward. And the least I could do was create an event to connect designers together.

Curiosity Driven

Another important lesson I’ve learned in my courses is curiosity. Curiosity time and time again has paid off in class and the lessons I’ve learned from having to go explore topics and design my own projects. Not just stopping at the core readings, but asking why something works the way it is and developing my own sources of argument and verification. Sometimes, it is crucial to question why things are working the way it is. You might have a different perspective and even though it might not be right, it’ll lead to discussions and potential flaws of the readings. Nothing is set in stone “the truth”. Facts will always change and that is the beauty of a university, a space to challenge the things we learn.


Asking why has led me to do some amazing things. In my Sophomore and Junior years, I participated in a number of UW-hosted hackathons and won a few. Hackathons gave me an opportunity to be curious within a community of hackers. By bouncing ideas back and forth with my peers, I was able to grow in my learnings and create clever solutions for water inefficiencies and remote learning. This aspect of ideating as well as creating some sort of software was my passion and something that has guided me to my professional journey of being a software engineer.


Landing a role in tech was more than just having the technical components, it was also about having professional skills.

Value of Professionalism

It wasn’t until taking INFO 386, a professionalism class, where I realized how crucial this skill was. Some might say life-changing because how you uphold yourself is how others will treat you. And after refining my speaking, writing, and presentation skills, I saw a complete 180 in how I was able to articulate myself.


This skill was extremely useful in my Capstone class where I worked with the WA Chamber of Commerce to create a funding dashboard application for small businesses. Here, I had numerous meetings with executive-level stakeholders who had various different expectations on the direction of the project. Managing all these expectations and communicating technical nuances to non-technical individuals was daunting. Luckily, with the professionalism skills learned in INFO 386, I was able to impress the project stakeholders and create a meaningful project.
